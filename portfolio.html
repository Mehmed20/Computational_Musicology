Anime Openings vs Endings

A Computational Musicology Analysis
AuthorMehmed InstitutionUniversiteit van Amsterdam DateMarch 29, 2026
Computational Musicology 115215146Y · Spring 2026
Instructor: John Ashley Burgoyne

This portfolio investigates whether anime opening and ending tracks exhibit measurable differences in computational audio features. Using Spotify's track-level features, chroma analysis, and machine learning, we test whether the perceived energy and structural differences translate into numerical evidence.

Can We Measure the Difference?

Anime openings are designed to excite and energize. Endings are meant to be contemplative and conclusive. This project asks a simple question: can computational audio features detect this difference?

Using data from 200+ anime opening tracks and 57+ ending tracks pulled from Spotify, we combine track-level audio features (Energy, Tempo, Loudness) with frame-level chroma analysis from Sonic Visualiser to build a complete picture of how these genres differ acoustically.

▸ Central Research Question

Do computational music features reliably distinguish anime openings from endings, and which acoustic dimensions contribute most to that classification?

Background & Corpus

Openings

The opening playlist contains 200 anime opening tracks (OPs). These are typically fast, energetic, and designed to grab attention in the first 90 seconds of an episode. They feature high-energy production, punchy kicks, and lead synths or vocals meant to excite the listener.

Endings

The ending playlist contains 57 anime ending tracks (EDs). These are slower, more melodic, and designed to leave the viewer with a thoughtful or bittersweet impression. They often feature acoustic instruments, softer vocals, and longer instrumental builds.

Focus tracks for detailed analysis include Just Awake (appears in both playlists at 191 BPM as opening and 95.5 BPM as ending), Gats (extreme outlier: Energy 0.193), and several representative examples from each category.

Chroma Features (Pitch Analysis)

Chroma features represent the distribution of energy across the 12 pitch classes (C, C#, D, etc.). Using Sonic Visualiser's NNLS Chroma extraction, we analyzed frame-level pitch content for focus tracks.

Just Awake – Opening (191 BPM)

NNLS Chroma analysis showing dominant pitch classes over time. Color intensity = spectral energy.

Just Awake – Ending (95.5 BPM)

Same harmonic content, double duration. Note the smoother pitch evolution in the slower version.

Finding: Dominant pitch classes (C, G#, D#) form a C-minor triad, characteristic of metalcore. The opening's higher tempo creates more frequent harmonic shifts, while the ending sustains chords longer.

Classification Model

To quantify the predictive power of these features, we trained a logistic regression classifier on a stratified 80/20 train-test split (accounting for class imbalance).

Both models achieve similar accuracy. Feature selection provides a more parsimonious model.

Interpretation: The feature-selected model achieves comparable accuracy with fewer features, suggesting that Energy, Danceability, Mode, and Duration alone are sufficient to distinguish opening from ending with ~85% reliability.

Conclusions & Implications

This analysis confirms that anime openings and endings are not just subjectively different—they are objectively measurable as distinct audio feature profiles. The differences span multiple dimensions:

  • Tempo: Openings are 39 BPM faster on average
  • Loudness: Openings are 3.6 dB louder (a substantial perceptual difference)
  • Energy: Openings score 0.16 higher (16% more energetic)
  • Instrumentation: Endings are more acoustic and instrumental
  • Rhythm: Openings are significantly more "danceable"

A simple logistic regression classifier achieves 84.62% accuracy, with just four features (Energy, Danceability, Mode, Duration) sufficient for reliable classification.

Implications for Music Supervisors and Composers

These findings suggest that opening and ending music are produced under different aesthetic constraints. Composers intentionally choose higher tempos, louder mixes, and more synthetic instrumentation for openings. This portfolio demonstrates that those choices leave a measurable fingerprint in the Spotify feature space.

Future Work

Future analyses could explore genre subdivisions within anime music (metal, electronic, orchestral endings), temporal evolution within a track (intro vs. chorus intensity), or cross-cultural comparison with opening/ending conventions in Western television.
